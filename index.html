About

The World’s First National Park

Established on March 1, 1872, Yellowstone National Park spans more than 2.2 million acres across Wyoming, Montana, and Idaho — making it larger than Rhode Island and Delaware combined. Sitting atop one of the world’s largest active volcanic systems, the park is home to more geothermal features than anywhere else on Earth: over 10,000 hydrothermal sites including fumaroles, mud pots, hot springs, and geysers.

Beyond its geological wonders, Yellowstone supports a remarkable ecosystem. Its vast forests, meadows, rivers, and canyons shelter an extraordinary array of wildlife — from bison herds roaming the Lamar Valley to wolves, grizzly bears, and pronghorn. The park’s biodiversity makes it one of the most important conservation areas in North America.

Featured Areas

  • 01

    Old Faithful & Upper Geyser Basin

    Home to the world’s most famous geyser, Old Faithful erupts approximately every 90 minutes, shooting 3,700–8,400 gallons of boiling water up to 185 feet in the air. The surrounding Upper Geyser Basin contains the highest concentration of geysers on the planet.

  • 02

    Grand Prismatic Spring

    At 370 feet wide and over 121 feet deep, Grand Prismatic is the largest hot spring in the United States and the third largest in the world. Its vivid rings of orange, yellow, and green are produced by heat-adapted microorganisms called thermophiles.

  • 03

    Lamar Valley

    Known as the “Serengeti of North America,” Lamar Valley offers some of the best wildlife viewing in the world. Visitors regularly spot bison, elk, pronghorn, black bears, grizzlies, and the reintroduced wolf packs that roam the valley floor.

  • 04

    Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone

    The Yellowstone River carved this dramatic 20-mile canyon through volcanic rock, creating walls of vivid yellow, orange, and red. The 308-foot Lower Falls — nearly twice the height of Niagara — is one of the park’s most iconic sights.
